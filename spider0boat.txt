      <p>A <b>boat</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Watercraft" title="Watercraft">watercraft</a> of a large range of types and sizes, but generally smaller than a <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ship</a>, which is distinguished by its larger size, shape, cargo or passenger capacity, or its ability to carry boats. </p>
      <p>Small boats are typically found on inland waterways such as <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a>, or in protected coastal areas. However, some boats, such as the <a href="/wiki/Whaleboat" title="Whaleboat">whaleboat</a>, were intended for use in an offshore environment. In modern <a href="/wiki/Navy" title="Navy">naval</a> terms, a boat is a vessel small enough to be carried aboard a ship.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Boats vary in proportion and construction methods with their intended purpose, available materials, or local traditions. <a href="/wiki/Canoe" title="Canoe">Canoes</a> have been used since prehistoric times and remain in use throughout the world for transportation, fishing, and sport. <a href="/wiki/Fishing_boat" class="mw-redirect" title="Fishing boat">Fishing boats</a> vary widely in style partly to match local conditions. <a href="/wiki/Pleasure_boat" class="mw-redirect" title="Pleasure boat">Pleasure craft</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Boating" title="Boating">recreational boating</a> include ski boats, <a href="/wiki/Pontoon_(boat)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pontoon (boat)">pontoon boats</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sailboat" title="Sailboat">sailboats</a>. <a href="/wiki/House_boat" class="mw-redirect" title="House boat">House boats</a> may be used for vacationing or long-term residence. <a href="/wiki/Lighter_(barge)" title="Lighter (barge)">Lighters</a> are used to convey cargo to and from large ships unable to get close to shore. <a href="/wiki/Lifeboat_(shipboard)" title="Lifeboat (shipboard)">Lifeboats</a> have rescue and safety functions. </p>
      <p>Boats can be propelled by manpower (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Rowboat" class="mw-redirect" title="Rowboat">rowboats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pedalo" title="Pedalo">paddle boats</a>), wind (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Sailboat" title="Sailboat">sailboats</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Inboard_motor" title="Inboard motor">inboard</a>/<a href="/wiki/Outboard_motor" title="Outboard motor">outboard</a> <a href="/wiki/Motor" class="mw-redirect" title="Motor">motors</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Gasoline_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Gasoline engine">gasoline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric</a>). </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Silver_model_of_a_boat,_tomb_PG_789,_Royal_Cemetery_of_UR,_2600-2500_BCE.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Silver_model_of_a_boat%2C_tomb_PG_789%2C_Royal_Cemetery_of_UR%2C_2600-2500_BCE.jpg/220px-Silver_model_of_a_boat%2C_tomb_PG_789%2C_Royal_Cemetery_of_UR%2C_2600-2500_BCE.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="90" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Silver_model_of_a_boat%2C_tomb_PG_789%2C_Royal_Cemetery_of_UR%2C_2600-2500_BCE.jpg/330px-Silver_model_of_a_boat%2C_tomb_PG_789%2C_Royal_Cemetery_of_UR%2C_2600-2500_BCE.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Silver_model_of_a_boat%2C_tomb_PG_789%2C_Royal_Cemetery_of_UR%2C_2600-2500_BCE.jpg/440px-Silver_model_of_a_boat%2C_tomb_PG_789%2C_Royal_Cemetery_of_UR%2C_2600-2500_BCE.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1922" data-file-height="790"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Silver_model_of_a_boat,_tomb_PG_789,_Royal_Cemetery_of_UR,_2600-2500_BCE.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>Silver model of a boat, tomb PG 789, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Cemetery_of_Ur" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Cemetery of Ur">Royal Cemetery of Ur</a>, 2600-2500 BCE.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>After the <a href="/wiki/Homo_erectus" title="Homo erectus">Homo erectus</a> possibly using <a href="/wiki/Watercraft" title="Watercraft">watercrafts</a> more than a million years ago crossing <a href="/wiki/Strait" title="Strait">straits</a> between landmasses,<sup id="cite_ref-Te_Ara_Encyclopedia_of_New_Zealand_–_Te_Ara_Encyclopedia_of_New_Zealand_2019_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Te_Ara_Encyclopedia_of_New_Zealand_–_Te_Ara_Encyclopedia_of_New_Zealand_2019-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Vaucher_2012_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vaucher_2012-3">[3]</a></sup> boats have served as transportation far into the <a href="/wiki/Pre-historic" class="mw-redirect" title="Pre-historic">pre-historic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-d1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-d1-4">[4]</a></sup> Circumstantial evidence, such as the <a href="/wiki/Early_human_migrations#Near_Oceania" title="Early human migrations">early settlement of Australia</a> over 40,000 years ago, findings in <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a> dated 130,000 years ago,<sup id="cite_ref-Plakias2010_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Plakias2010-5">[5]</a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Flores" title="Flores">Flores</a> dated to 900,000 years ago,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> suggest that boats have been used since prehistoric times. The earliest boats are thought to have been <a href="/wiki/Dugout_(boat)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dugout (boat)">dugouts</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-McGrail11_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGrail11-7">[7]</a></sup> and the oldest boats found by archaeological excavation date from around 7,000–10,000 years ago. The oldest recovered boat in the world, the <a href="/wiki/Pesse_canoe" title="Pesse canoe">Pesse canoe</a>, found in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, is a dugout made from the hollowed tree trunk of a <i><a href="/wiki/Pinus_sylvestris" title="Pinus sylvestris">Pinus sylvestris</a></i> that was constructed somewhere between 8200 and 7600 BC. This <a href="/wiki/Canoe" title="Canoe">canoe</a> is exhibited in the Drents Museum in Assen, Netherlands.<sup id="cite_ref-Van_der_Heide_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Van_der_Heide-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Boat_of_Pesse_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boat_of_Pesse-9">[9]</a></sup> Other very old dugout boats have also been recovered.<sup id="cite_ref-chinaorg2002_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chinaorg2002-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-McGrail431_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGrail431-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-italy2005_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-italy2005-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Raft" title="Raft">Rafts</a> have operated for at least 8,000 years.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> A 7,000-year-old seagoing <a href="/wiki/Reed_boat" title="Reed boat">reed boat</a> has been found at <a href="/wiki/H3_(Kuwait)" title="H3 (Kuwait)">site H3</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawler2002_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawler2002-14">[14]</a></sup> Boats were used between 4000 and 3000 BC in <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumer</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-d1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-d1-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a><sup id="cite_ref-McGrail17_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGrail17-15">[15]</a></sup> and in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-d1_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-d1-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Boats played an important role in the commerce between the <a href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Indus Valley Civilization">Indus Valley Civilization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-McGrail251_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGrail251-16">[16]</a></sup> Evidence of varying models of boats has also been discovered at various Indus Valley archaeological sites.<sup id="cite_ref-McGrail_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGrail-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Uru_(boat)" title="Uru (boat)">Uru</a> craft originate in <a href="/wiki/Beypore" title="Beypore">Beypore</a>, a village in south <a href="/wiki/Kozhikode" title="Kozhikode">Calicut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a>, in southwestern <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. This type of mammoth wooden ship was constructed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (November 2015)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> solely of teak, with a transport capacity of 400 tonnes. The ancient Arabs and Greeks used such boats as trading vessels.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The historians <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> record the use of boats for commerce, travel, and military purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-McGrail_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGrail-17">[17]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span></h2>

      <p>Boats can be categorized into three main types: </p> 
      <ol>
       <li>Unpowered or human-powered. Unpowered craft include <a href="/wiki/Raft" title="Raft">rafts</a> meant for one-way downstream travel. Human-powered boats include <a href="/wiki/Canoe" title="Canoe">canoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kayak" title="Kayak">kayaks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gondola" title="Gondola">gondolas</a> and boats propelled by poles like a <a href="/wiki/Punt_(boat)" title="Punt (boat)">punt</a>.</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Sailboat" title="Sailboat">Sailboats</a>, propelled mainly by means of <a href="/wiki/Sail" title="Sail">sails</a>.</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Motorboat" title="Motorboat">Motorboats</a>, propelled by mechanical means, such as <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">engines</a>.</li>
      </ol> 
      <p>A number of large vessels are usually referred to as boats. <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">Submarines</a> are a prime example.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Other types of large vessels which are traditionally called boats include <a href="/wiki/Lake_freighter" title="Lake freighter">Great Lakes freighters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Riverboat" title="Riverboat">riverboats</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ferryboat" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferryboat">ferryboats</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams-21">[21]</a></sup> Though large enough to carry their own boats and heavy cargoes, these vessels are designed for operation on inland or protected coastal waters. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span></h2> 
      <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096">
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Further information: <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_nautical_terms_(A-L)" title="Glossary of nautical terms (A-L)">Glossary of nautical terms (A-L)</a>
      </div> 
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Hull_(watercraft)" title="Hull (watercraft)">hull</a> is the main, and in some cases only, structural component of a boat. It provides both capacity and <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Keel" title="Keel">keel</a> is a boat's "backbone", a lengthwise structural member to which the perpendicular frames are fixed. On most boats a <a href="/wiki/Deck_(ship)" title="Deck (ship)">deck</a> covers the hull, in part or whole. While a <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ship</a> often has several decks, a boat is unlikely to have more than one. Above the deck are often <a href="/wiki/Lifeline_(safety)" title="Lifeline (safety)">lifelines</a> connected to <a href="/wiki/Stanchion" title="Stanchion">stanchions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulwark_(nautical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bulwark (nautical)">bulwarks</a> perhaps topped by <a href="/wiki/Gunnel_(ship_element)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gunnel (ship element)">gunnels</a>, or some combination of the two. A <a href="/wiki/Cabin_(ship)" title="Cabin (ship)">cabin</a> may protrude above the deck forward, aft, along the centerline, or covering much of the length of the boat. Vertical structures dividing the internal spaces are known as <a href="/wiki/Bulkhead_(partition)" title="Bulkhead (partition)">bulkheads</a>. </p>
      <p>The forward end of a boat is called the <a href="/wiki/Bow_(ship)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bow (ship)">bow</a>, the aft end the <a href="/wiki/Stern" title="Stern">stern</a>. Facing forward the right side is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Starboard" class="mw-redirect" title="Starboard">starboard</a> and the left side as <a href="/wiki/Port_and_starboard" title="Port and starboard">port</a>. </p>

      <p>Until the mid-19th century most boats were made of natural materials, primarily wood, although <a href="/wiki/Reed_(plant)" title="Reed (plant)">reed</a>, bark and animal skins were also used. Early boats include the bound-reed style of boat seen in Ancient Egypt, the <a href="/wiki/Birch_bark" title="Birch bark">birch bark</a> <a href="/wiki/Canoe" title="Canoe">canoe</a>, the animal hide-covered <a href="/wiki/Kayak" title="Kayak">kayak</a><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Coracle" title="Coracle">coracle</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dugout_canoe" title="Dugout canoe">dugout canoe</a> made from a single log. </p>
      <p>By the mid-19th century, many boats had been built with iron or steel frames but still planked in wood. In 1855 <a href="/wiki/Ferro-cement" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferro-cement">ferro-cement</a> boat construction was patented by the French, who coined the name "ferciment". This is a system by which a steel or iron wire framework is built in the shape of a boat's hull and covered over with cement. Reinforced with bulkheads and other internal structure it is strong but heavy, easily repaired, and, if sealed properly, will not leak or corrode.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>As the forests of Britain and Europe continued to be over-harvested to supply the keels of larger wooden boats, and the <a href="/wiki/Bessemer_process" title="Bessemer process">Bessemer process</a> (<a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patented</a> in 1855) cheapened the cost of steel, steel ships and boats began to be more common. By the 1930s boats built entirely of steel from frames to plating were seen replacing wooden boats in many industrial uses and fishing fleets. Private recreational boats of steel remain uncommon. In 1895 WH Mullins produced steel boats of galvanized iron and by 1930 became the world's largest producer of pleasure boats. </p>
      <p>Mullins also offered boats in aluminum from 1895 through 1899 and once again in the 1920s,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> but it wasn't until the mid-20th century that <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> gained widespread popularity. Though much more expensive than steel, aluminum alloys exist that do not corrode in salt water, allowing a similar load carrying capacity to steel at much less weight. </p>
      <p>Around the mid-1960s, boats made of <a href="/wiki/Fiberglass" title="Fiberglass">fiberglass</a> (aka "glassfibre") became popular, especially for recreational boats. Fiberglass is also known as "GRP" (glass-reinforced plastic) in the UK, and "FRP" (for fiber-reinforced plastic) in the US. Fiberglass boats are strong, and do not rust, corrode, or rot. Instead, they are susceptible to structural degradation from sunlight and extremes in temperature over their lifespan. Fiberglass structures can be made stiffer with sandwich panels, where the fiberglass encloses a lightweight core such as balsa<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> or foam. </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Cold_moulding" class="mw-redirect" title="Cold moulding">Cold moulding</a> is a modern construction method, using wood as the structural component. In cold moulding very thin strips of wood are layered over a form. Each layer is coated with resin, followed by another directionally alternating layer laid on top. Subsequent layers may be stapled or otherwise mechanically fastened to the previous, or weighted or vacuum bagged to provide compression and stabilization until the resin sets. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Buoyancy">Buoyancy</span></h2> 
      <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096">
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">Buoyancy</a>
      </div> 
      <p>A boat <a href="/wiki/Displacement_(fluid)" title="Displacement (fluid)">displaces</a> its weight in water, regardless whether it is made of wood, steel, fiberglass, or even concrete. If weight is added to the boat, the volume of the hull drawn below the waterline will increase to keep the balance above and below the surface equal. Boats have a natural or designed level of buoyancy. Exceeding it will cause the boat first to ride lower in the water, second to take on water more readily than when properly loaded, and ultimately, if overloaded by any combination of structure, cargo, and water, sink. </p>
      <p>As commercial vessels must be correctly loaded to be safe, and as the sea becomes less buoyant in brackish areas such as the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Waterline" title="Waterline">Plimsoll line</a> was introduced to prevent overloading. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Union_classification">European Union classification</span></h2> 
      <p>Since 1998 all new leisure boats and barges built in Europe between 2.5m and 24m must comply with the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Recreational_Craft_Directive" title="Recreational Craft Directive">Recreational Craft Directive</a> (RCD). The Directive establishes four categories that permit the allowable <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">wind</a> and wave conditions for vessels in each class:<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Class A - the boat may safely navigate any waters.</li> 
       <li>Class B - the boat is limited to offshore navigation. (Winds up to Force 8 &amp; waves up to 4 metres)</li> 
       <li>Class C - the boat is limited to inshore (coastal) navigation. (Winds up to Force 6 &amp; waves up to 2 metres)</li> 
       <li>Class D - the boat is limited to rivers, canals and small lakes. (Winds up to Force 4 &amp; waves up to 0.5 metres)</li>
      </ul>
