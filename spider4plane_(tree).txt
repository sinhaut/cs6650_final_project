      <p><i><b>Platanus</b></i> <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESunset_Western_Garden_Book1995_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESunset_Western_Garden_Book1995-1">[1]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> consisting of a small number of <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">tree</a> species native to the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>. They are the sole living members of the family <a href="/wiki/Platanaceae" title="Platanaceae">Platanaceae</a>. </p>
      <p>All mature members of <i>Platanus</i> are tall, reaching 30–50&nbsp;m (98–164&nbsp;ft) in height. All except for <i>P. kerrii</i> are <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a>, and most are found in <a href="/wiki/Riparian" class="mw-redirect" title="Riparian">riparian</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetland</a> <a href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Habitat (ecology)">habitats</a> in the wild, though proving <a href="/wiki/Drought-tolerant" class="mw-redirect" title="Drought-tolerant">drought-tolerant</a> in cultivation. The hybrid <a href="/wiki/London_plane" class="mw-redirect" title="London plane">London plane</a> (<i>Platanus </i>×<i> acerifolia</i>) has proved particularly tolerant of urban conditions, and has been widely planted in London and elsewhere in the United Kingdom. </p>
      <p>They are often known in English as <i><b>planes</b></i> or <i><b>plane trees</b></i>. A formerly used name that is now rare is <i>plantain tree</i> (not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Plantain_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Plantain (disambiguation)">other, unrelated, species with the name</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Some North American species are called <i>sycamores</i> (especially <i><a href="/wiki/Platanus_occidentalis" title="Platanus occidentalis">Platanus occidentalis</a></i>),<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMerriam_Webster_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMerriam_Webster-3">[3]</a></sup> although the term is also used for <a href="/wiki/Sycamore" title="Sycamore">several unrelated species of trees</a>. The genus name <i>Platanus</i> comes from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">πλάτανος</span></span>, which referred to <i><a href="/wiki/Platanus_orientalis" title="Platanus orientalis">Platanus orientalis</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>The flowers are reduced and are borne in balls (globose heads); 3–7 hairy <a href="/wiki/Sepal" title="Sepal">sepals</a> may be fused at the base, and the petals are 3–7 and are <a href="/wiki/Leaf_shape" class="mw-redirect" title="Leaf shape">spatulate</a>. Male and female flowers are separate, but borne on the same plant (<a href="/wiki/Monoecy" title="Monoecy">monoecious</a>). The number of heads in one cluster (<a href="/wiki/Inflorescence" title="Inflorescence">inflorescence</a>) is indicative of the species (see table below). The male flower has 3–8 <a href="/wiki/Stamen" title="Stamen">stamens</a>; the female has a superior ovary with 3–7 <a href="/wiki/Carpel" class="mw-redirect" title="Carpel">carpels</a>. Plane trees are wind-pollinated. Male flower-heads fall off after shedding their pollen. </p>
      <p>After being pollinated, the female flowers become <a href="/wiki/Achene" title="Achene">achenes</a> that form an aggregate ball. The fruit is a multiple of achenes (plant systematics, Simpson M. G., 2006). Typically, the core of the ball is 1&nbsp;cm in diameter and is covered with a net of mesh 1&nbsp;mm, which can be peeled off. The ball is 2.5–4&nbsp;cm in diameter and contains several hundred achenes, each of which has a single seed and is conical, with the point attached downward to the net at the surface of the ball. There is also a tuft of many thin stiff yellow-green bristle fibers attached to the base of each achene. These bristles help in wind dispersion of the fruits as in the <a href="/wiki/Dandelion" class="mw-redirect" title="Dandelion">dandelion</a>. </p>
      <p>The leaves are <a href="/wiki/Leaf_shape" class="mw-redirect" title="Leaf shape">simple</a> and alternate. In the subgenus <i>Platanus</i> they have a palmate outline. The base of the leaf stalk (petiole) is enlarged and completely wraps around the young stem bud in its axil. The axillary bud is exposed only after the leaf falls off. </p>
      <p>The mature bark peels off or exfoliates easily in irregularly shaped patches, producing a mottled, scaly appearance. On old trunks, bark may not flake off, but thickens and cracks instead. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phylogeny">Phylogeny</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Platanus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Phylogeny">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>There are two subgenera, subgenus <i>Castaneophyllum</i> containing the anomalous <i><a href="/wiki/Platanus_kerrii" title="Platanus kerrii">P. kerrii</a></i>, and subgenus <i>Platanus</i>, with all the others; recent studies in Mexico<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENixonPoole2003_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENixonPoole2003-5">[5]</a></sup> have increased the number of accepted species in this subgenus. Within subgenus <i>Platanus</i>, genetic evidence suggests that <i>P. racemosa</i> is more closely related to <i>P. orientalis</i> than it is to the other North American species.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFengOhManos2005_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFengOhManos2005-6">[6]</a></sup> The fossil record of leaves and fruit identifiable to <i>Plantanus</i> begins in the <a href="/wiki/Paleocene" title="Paleocene">Paleocene</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MissisippiMiocene_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MissisippiMiocene-7">[7]</a></sup> Despite the geographic separation between North America and Old World, species from these continents will cross readily resulting in fertile <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrids</a> such as the London plane. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diseases">Diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Platanus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">

         </div>Severe infections of anthracnose can sometimes defoliate large swaths of American sycamore forest during mid and late spring, but trees generally recover by mid-summer
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096">
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Platanus_diseases" title="List of Platanus diseases">List of Platanus diseases</a>
      </div> 
      <p>Planes are susceptible to plane anthracnose (<i>Apiognomonia veneta</i>), a <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungal</a> disease that can defoliate the trees in some years. The most severe infections are associated with cold, wet spring weather. <i>P. occidentalis</i> and the other American species are the most susceptible, with <i>P. orientalis</i> the most resistant. The hybrid London plane is intermediate in resistance. </p>
      <p><i><a href="/wiki/Ceratocystis_platani" title="Ceratocystis platani">Ceratocystis platani</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Wilt_disease" title="Wilt disease">wilt disease</a>, has become a significant problem in recent years in much of Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPathology_note_72008_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPathology_note_72008-8">[8]</a></sup> The North American species are mostly resistant to the disease, with which they probably coevolved, while the old world species are highly sensitive. </p>
      <p>Other diseases such as <a href="/wiki/Powdery_mildew" title="Powdery mildew">powdery mildew</a> occur frequently, but are of lesser importance. </p>
      <p><i>Platanus</i> species are used as food plants by the <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> of some <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">Lepidoptera</a> species including <i><a href="/wiki/Phyllonorycter_platani" title="Phyllonorycter platani">Phyllonorycter platani</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Setaceous_Hebrew_Character" class="mw-redirect" title="Setaceous Hebrew Character">Setaceous Hebrew Character</a>. </p>
      <p>In the 21st century a disease, commonly known as Massaria disease, has attacked plane trees across Europe. It is caused by the fungus <i><a href="/wiki/Splanchnonema_platani" title="Splanchnonema platani">Splanchnonema platani</a></i>, and causes large lesions on the upper sides of branches.<sup id="cite_ref-fc_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fc-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-tt_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tt-10">[10]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Platanus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The principal use of these trees is as ornamental trees, especially in urban areas and by roadsides. The <a href="/wiki/London_plane" class="mw-redirect" title="London plane">London plane</a> is particularly popular for this purpose. The <a href="/wiki/American_plane" class="mw-redirect" title="American plane">American plane</a> is cultivated sometimes for timber and investigations have been made into its use as a <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> crop. The <a href="/wiki/Oriental_plane" class="mw-redirect" title="Oriental plane">oriental plane</a> is widely used as an ornamental and also has a number of minor medicinal uses. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_history">Cultural history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Platanus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cultural history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Plane_Tree_bark_at_Tayac,_Dordogne.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Plane_Tree_bark_at_Tayac%2C_Dordogne.jpg/170px-Plane_Tree_bark_at_Tayac%2C_Dordogne.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Plane_Tree_bark_at_Tayac%2C_Dordogne.jpg/255px-Plane_Tree_bark_at_Tayac%2C_Dordogne.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Plane_Tree_bark_at_Tayac%2C_Dordogne.jpg/340px-Plane_Tree_bark_at_Tayac%2C_Dordogne.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1920" data-file-height="2560"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Plane_Tree_bark_at_Tayac,_Dordogne.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div><a href="/wiki/Patterns_in_nature" title="Patterns in nature">Patterned</a> bark of <a href="/wiki/London_plane" class="mw-redirect" title="London plane">London plane</a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>Most significant aspects of cultural history apply to <i><a href="/wiki/Platanus_orientalis#Cultural_history" title="Platanus orientalis">Platanus orientalis</a></i> in the Old World. The tree is an important part of the literary scenery of Plato's dialogue <a href="/wiki/Phaedrus_(dialogue)" title="Phaedrus (dialogue)">Phaedrus</a>. Because of <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>, the tree also played an important role in the scenery of Cicero's <a href="/wiki/De_Oratore" title="De Oratore">De Oratore</a>. The trees also provided the shade under which <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> and Plato’s famed philosophical schools were held.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Handel" class="mw-redirect" title="Handel">Handel</a>'s opera <a href="/wiki/Serse" title="Serse">Serse</a> features a famous aria, "<a href="/wiki/Ombra_mai_fu" title="Ombra mai fu">Ombra mai fu</a>," which the title character sings in praise of his favorite plane tree. </p>
      <p>The plane tree has been a frequent motif featured in <a href="/wiki/Classical_Chinese_poetry" title="Classical Chinese poetry">Classical Chinese poetry</a> as an embodiment of sorrowful sentiments due to its autumnal shedding of leaves. </p>
      <p>The legendary <a href="/wiki/Dry_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Dry tree">Dry tree</a> first recorded by <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a> was possibly a platanus. According to the legend, it marked the site of the battle between <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> and <a href="/wiki/Darius_III" title="Darius III">Darius III</a>. </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/German_World_War_II_camouflage_patterns" title="German World War II camouflage patterns">German camouflage pattern</a> <i>Platanenmuster</i> ("plane tree pattern"), designed in 1937–1942 by Johann Georg Otto Schick, was the first dotted camouflage pattern.<sup id="cite_ref-Beaver_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beaver-12">[12]</a></sup> </p>
